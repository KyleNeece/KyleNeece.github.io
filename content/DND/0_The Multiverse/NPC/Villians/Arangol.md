##### Personality  
Arangol is a power hungry sadistic manipulator. He is described as an embodiement of evil, albeit to a lesser extend than the [[The Istoria Rytsarey]]. His ultimate goal is to kill the Omnipotent One, **(add about his own sealing)** taking his place and commanding a self-built an army that would conquest the universe under absolute authority. To achieve this, he sought to harness the power of one or multiple objects (if he can), one being the orb (from Kindrack Diablo), another option being Jaloop's fragment of the Omnipotent One, and the final option book of narratives, all reality manipulating artifacts. He believes that these items can grant him divine and old one powers, elevating him above all mortals and ascending him to godhood. Arangol is highly condescending towards others; whenever confronted he is casual and uncaring, attempting to draw out conversations and make small talk. His pursuit of power is marked by intense pragmatism, as he sacrifices many lives to the Book of Narratives in attempt to unleash its full power. Despite his sadistic tendencies, Arangol doesn't kill indiscriminately as he typically warns others to not over step in attempt to stop him. 
Arangol's most defining trait is his arrogance which is outwardly shown as a god complex, that grows even further after merging with the **Book of Narratives** (He declares that no one besides him, not even the gods, has ever "stood upon the heavens" and believes himself superior to all in all aspects). He rarely shows any sign of alarm or distress, dismissing even the most powerful of opponents. However, Arangol's overconfidence and arrogance is his weakness, as it blinds him to failure. Whenever he is outmatched, he grows enraged when he realizes he is losing against a being he views as lesser. Arangol cannot comprehend how a mortal can match him showing his inability to recognize his own limitations, questioning why someone as brilliant as him would allow himself to be controlled by the Omnipotent One. This shows his belief in absolute independence and rejection of higher powers.
It is also hinted at that Arangol's obsession of his own power stems from a longing to be be recognized, as those emotionally intelligent enough can feel the loneliness seeping through his spells and martial combat. This brings the theory that the loss of power from the **Book of Narratives** was due to a hidden desire to be an ordinary person, born from the isolation of possessing extraordinary powers with out anyone able to understand his perspective, which the Book of Narratives gave him. 
Despite his arrogance, Arangol is still capable of showing respect towards others for their capabilities. He is rather humble when it comes to earning praise himself, often brushing off compliments about his praiseworthy actions. Arangol is also highly cautious, and often has entire situations planned out well in advance, as shown throughout the entire Felwinter Saga by his puppeteering control over events. However, per Arangol's own acknolwegdement, he had become far less cautious after merging with the **Book of Narratives**
Arangol is highly manipulative and exploits the trust and emotions of those around him. He also displays barbaric cruelty and hypocrisy, taunting those in powerless positions and tells those who overpower him (pretty much no instances) to not get full of themselves for overpowering him in battle, despite his own arrogance. Arangol believes winners must dictate how the world should be, dismissing explanations of the role The Omnipotent One plays in the universe as the logic of a loser. Moreover, Arangol has a cynical view of mortal characteristics, seeing love and respect as the furthest state from understanding and trust as a cowardly reliance. To him, mortals depend on superiors out of blind faith, leading to the creation of kings and gods. Arangol believes only in harsh realities and denies the existence of truth and lies do not exist, believing that and that weak people cling to convenient untruths to avoid painful facts.
Arangol expresses particular faith in his forces, reassuring that they cannot be defeated as long as they stand together. He treats his subordinates as comrades and is polite, but is also harsh in disciplining. However, Arangol does not immediately punish all disobedience, and sometimes he instead shows understanding of certain actions. 
Ultimately though, Arangol views his subordinates as tools to be used and discarded at his convenience. Furthermore, he shows no mercy to those who betray him, and will ruthlessly cut them down or punish them for doing so. He has a willingness to exploit others is further exemplified by exploiting his forces and others under his command throughout the saga. Although he perceives his subordinates as disposable, he is still capable of expressing gratitude towards them, often thanking them for deeds that benefit himself. 
Arangol exhibits a deep prejudice against all mortals. He is visibly angered by the idea of being surpassed by a mere mortal and claims that **The Book of Narratives** cannot stand to see a one overpower him.
