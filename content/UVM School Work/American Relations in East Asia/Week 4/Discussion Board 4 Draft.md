From the historical experience of Americans in East Asia from the 1840's until the 1970's, we have learned the progression of the relationship between the Americans and the East Asians. It started as violent colonial imperialism and superiority, but transitioned into a more understanding and amending relationship. 

During week 1, we looked at how the East Asian countries were opened up by American representatives. 

First came the American Missionaries who were representatives from the American Board of Commissioners for Foreign Missions. E.C. Bridgman, the leader of such group, arrived at the Canton Port, which began the earliest of Sino-American cultural and political relations ppp.2-3 pdf. This was also the time where the Opium trade comes into play. At first, the missionaries were concerned about the opium problem in China. It was stated to be "one of the greatest evils afflicting Chinese society" pp.4 pdf. However, Opium almost hit a point to where it was in consideration of being legalized by American Missionaries. The foreign citizens and missionaries in Shanghai were of the opinion that the legalization of it was preferable to the semi-legalized smuggling which was already in place pp.24. The legalization did not diminish the demand for Opium, it rather, increased it instead (pp27 of pdf). 
We also took a look Commodore Perry, the US naval officer who led an expedition to China in 1853. Perry's diplomacy succeeded partly because of the imposing fleet of warships and black ships, and also packing the ships with gifts and festive supplies. Perry and his crew's trip was in the spirit of Manifest Destiny: the idea that the US greatness was foreordained. It is the concept that the seat of civilization progressed westward at the urging of a dominant power, also called The arc of civilization (pp 4-5 pdf). Perry and his crew sought to impose cultural and societal values from Western culture and society onto the "'semi-barbarous' Japanese" (pp.6 pdf). 
The Missionaries and Perry were two examples of the first steps towards the the opening up East Asian countries to American relations, religion and trade, which was ultimately their goal at the time: To spread Christianity and to make lucrative gains.
