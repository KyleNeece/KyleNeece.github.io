**Start of Essay**

The transformation of Japan in American public opinion from brutal wartime enemy to peaceful postwar friend is not a clean break from the past. 


American perceptions of Japan in 1964 are still evident in their relations since the 1850s. 


Culture, race and gender shape perceptions of Japan in the 1960s to US citizens, which tells us a lot about American society during such time. If we look at the "First the Portuguese came, next, isolation - then Perry," it refers to U.S. Commodore Mathew Perry commanding the Black Ships, preparing to shoot to get better treatment for shipwrecked U.S. Sailors. This infers to us that American society felt as if they were the "good guys" and Perry was only there to protect the sailors. This however contradicts the narrative that the cartoons we saw brought to us. In the Cartoons we saw the U.S. trying to make deals as the "good guys" for economic gain, but then slowly descent into bringing out fire power and even a huge canon to shoot a "Beast" in the third Cartoon artwork. 
Furthermore, if we look into the "From Editorial The Unique Era, but What Now?", Edwin O. Reischauer's "Inevitable Partners", and the secondary source readings from this past week, we see some interesting correlations. Regarding the the Unique Era reading, it asks the question of “Can we, after what happened 20 years ago, trust the Japanese?” They ask this question yet they fail to mention what happened with the U.S. base expansion events during that respective period of time, when the Americans were lobbying to expand their military bases which in turn, disrupted the Japanese population and began ruining American to Japanese relations so much to the point where Eisenhower had to extract over fifty percent of the U.S. Soldiers from the country. In addition, in the "Unique Era" section of the magazine, they mention that Japan's biggest selling point at that time was their economy. This historically lines up with what America have been trying to get out of the East Asian countries: more trade and lucrative gain capability.  
Regarding  Edwin O. Reischauer's article in the Life magazine, he speaks only great things about American to Japanese interaction. He speaks about how in his interactions with the Japanese people, they are always courteous, kind, friendly, and many other positive virtues. What he fails to mention, is how the U.S. bases from the fourth secondary source we read this week threatened Japanese democracy which America was apparently trying to "help with", and how Japan's economy was largely started up again by the Korea war, including the demand of U.S. soldiers for Japanese females (as prostitutes). We could even delve further into the black market exchange, and other aspects but the main point being that Reischauer purposefully leaves these aspects out because he at a point intervened with the situation trying to minimize the bad effects that he didn't put any of into his article. 


The life magazine as a whole also gives a more optimistic outlook of the Japanese and American relationship, but if we look at the dates of the magazine versus the readings, we can see that the magazine was published in the nineteen sixties, and the readings were published in what looks to be the two thousands. This is important because during the sixties and around that time, Japanese studies were considered to be on the simpler side. It was only after the two thousands that scholars were able to dig deeper into what was going on in Japan during those historical time periods.
