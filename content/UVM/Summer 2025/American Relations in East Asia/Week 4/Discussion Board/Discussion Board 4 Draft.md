First came the American Missionaries who were representatives from the American Board of Commissioners for Foreign Missions. E.C. Bridgman, the leader of such group, arrived at the Canton Port, which began the earliest of Sino-American cultural and political relations pp.2-3 pdf. This was also around the time when the Opium trade in China driven by British military power came into play. At first, the missionaries were concerned about the opium problem in China. It was stated to be "one of the greatest evils afflicting Chinese society" pp.4 pdf. However, to further their Christian conversion mission the American Missionaries went so far as to favor legalizing opium to eliminate the charge that as Westerners they were associated with an illegal activity.  (The legalization did not diminish the demand for Opium; it rather increased it instead (pp27 of pdf). 

We also took a look at Commodore Perry, the US naval officer who led an expedition to China in 1853. Perry's diplomacy succeeded primarily because of the imposing fleet of warships (the black ships). But he also packed the ships with gifts and festive supplies that showed a presumptuous mission of cultural enlightenment. Perry and his crew's trip was in the spirit of Manifest Destiny: the idea that the US greatness was foreordained up to its West Coast and even beyond to the Western Pacific. It is the concept that the seat of civilization progressed westward at the urging of a dominant power, the US, also called the arc of civilization (pp 4-5 pdf). Perry and his crew sought to impose cultural, societal and even political values from Western culture and society onto the "'semi-barbarous' Japanese" (pp.6 pdf). 

The Missionaries and Perry were two examples of the first steps towards opening up East Asian countries to American relations, religion and trade. Their immediate goal at the time: To spread Christianity and to make lucrative gains. 

  

During Week 2, we looked at the different types of missionaries and their individual goals when abroad within the East Asian countries. They wanted to help these East Asian countries, but rooted in their subconscious was still a mindset of superiority. We have the Pagan Babies article, where the Pagan babies program was a bi-product of a “dismal” failure to convert adult Chinese to Catholicism.That was due to the Anti Christian and Foreign Tide during the 1920's and 30s, which in turn was due to European and US wars waged against China along with opium addiction and other problems associated with all foreigners, including the missionaries.

We also have the YMCA in Japan, which at first looked at budding Japanese colonialism as an enlightening positive force. But the Japanese, taking their cue from Perry’s military might and Western imperialism had begun to grow their nation to ultimately become nationalistic imperialists themselves. The situation was aggravated by western U.S. racial prejudice against Japanese immigrants, even extending to their exclusion. The result is Japan going to war with the US in 1941.

  

During Week 3, we transitioned our focus from the foreign missionaries and took a look at the overall post war relationship of the U.S. and the East Asian countries from the perspective of the locals, which was not a positive one. Sex, Race and Gender shaped U.S Military forces in post war East Asia by creating an American military culture of taking what they wanted and acting with a sense of superiority, which in turn drew a lot of anger towards themselves. In China for example, the U.S. military had stayed long after completing their goal of pushing out the Japanese forces in order to support the Chinese Nationalist war against the Chinese Communists.  Expecting to be treated “as Kings,”  American soldiers angered many Chinese people. American servicemen expressed Orientalist views, held racist and sexist attitudes toward Chinese women, took prostitution for granted, perpetrated sexual violence, and then benefited from immunity regarding local laws. 

In Korea and Japan, things went so far as the establishment of underground black markets, brothels and prostitution on a vast scale. While these activities did help jumpstart the economy, they also produced more cultural backlash from the respective East Asian countries. 

We also looked into Orientalism in Korea and the protest against bases in Japan. 

In Korea, U.S advisors felt superiority over the Korean people and as such, whenever there was a "mistake or weakness" during Korean training or assignments, Orientalism came into play.  Sometimes this resulted in a required change of behavior, e.g. no more banzai charges. But sometimes it resulted in permissiveness towards questionable behavior (“that’s just the way they are”) like corporal punishment of subordinates. 

In Japan, the U.S. base expansion angered many not just because of the noise, bad soldier behavior and taking of land. It also seemed like Japan's government began representing the interests of U.S. anticommunism over their own people, which caused protests among the citizens. In the end, Eisenhower decided to withdraw more than fifty percent of US troops from the country.

  

The withdrawal of the American troops from Japan was a turning point which showed that the Americans had begun to realize that their approach with the bases was a problem. The U.S. began to realize that their superior mindset and what they were bringing to the table regarding the development of East countries was not all good. Thus it would appear that the U.S. policy makers had become more sensitive to local feelings and were starting to change course in an attempt to build relations in a more mutually cooperative way. 

  

The best way to summarize all this is looking at Japan for instance. We start with Perry, who is pointing these massive gunships at Japan “for their own good,” which in turn throws Japan into a pattern of building their own military to wreak violence on their neighbors Korea and China. This eventually led to the Pacific War between Japan and the United States. Then followed the occupation of Japan which first concentrated on democratization of the country. But then, the rising threat of communism in Asia led to U.S. military intransigence about military base expansion.  Fierce Japanese local resistance then made Eisenhower and the rest of the U.S. realize that we are not as good as we thought we were.
