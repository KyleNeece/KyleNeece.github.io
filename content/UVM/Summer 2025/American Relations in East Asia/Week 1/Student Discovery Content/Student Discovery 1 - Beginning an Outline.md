Critics of the traditional Chinese examination system that was abolished in 1905 such as Parsons emphasize that the subjects being tested were not practical. What does such criticism fail to see?

Critics like William Barclay Parsons, focusing on "practicality" in the Western sense, often failed to see the multifaceted and deeply embedded purposes of the traditional Chinese examination system (Keju). While the exams certainly neglected modern scientific and technical knowledge, they served crucial functions within the imperial Chinese context:
 * Social Mobility and Meritocracy (in theory): The most celebrated aspect of the examination system was its promise of social mobility. Theoretically, any male, regardless of his family background, could rise to a high position in government by excelling in the exams. This offered a powerful incentive for study and created a sense of fairness and opportunity, even if in practice wealth and connections often provided an advantage (e.g., access to tutors, time for study). It was a unique system for its time in attempting to select officials based on merit rather than solely on aristocratic birth.
 * Maintaining Ideological Unity and Social Cohesion: The exams were almost exclusively based on the Confucian classics. This ensured that the ruling elite, drawn from all corners of the empire, shared a common intellectual and moral framework. This shared worldview was vital for maintaining social stability and a unified political culture across a vast and diverse empire. It instilled a set of values (filial piety, loyalty, righteousness, benevolence) that were considered essential for good governance and social order.
 * Strengthening Imperial Authority: By drawing officials from a wide pool and indoctrinating them in the Confucian classics, the emperor could cultivate a loyal bureaucracy that owed its position to the state and its system, rather than to powerful regional families or factions. This helped prevent the rise of independent power bases and reinforced the centralized authority of the imperial court.
 * Administrative Competence (within its context): While not "practical" in a modern scientific sense, the subjects tested were considered highly practical for governing a traditional agrarian empire. They emphasized moral reasoning, historical precedents, rhetorical skill, and the ability to compose formal documents—all crucial for bureaucratic administration, legal judgments, and diplomatic interactions in a pre-industrial society. The ability to articulate complex arguments, understand historical governance, and demonstrate mastery of classical language was seen as indicative of a person's intelligence, discipline, and moral character, which were deemed essential for effective leadership.
 * Cultural Preservation and Transmission: The examination system played a fundamental role in preserving and transmitting traditional Chinese culture, particularly Confucianism, across generations. It ensured that the core texts and values remained central to education and elite identity for over a thousand years.
 * A Counterweight to Aristocracy: In its early development, the examination system served as a powerful tool to weaken the power of hereditary aristocracies and promote a bureaucracy based on individual achievement. This was a significant innovation in statecraft.
By the late 19th century, as Western powers encroached and China faced internal and external crises, the limitations of the examination system became painfully clear. Its emphasis on classical knowledge over modern science, technology, and practical administration left China ill-equipped to compete in a rapidly changing world. Critics like Parsons, observing this decline through a Western lens of industrialization and scientific progress, naturally highlighted the lack of "practicality" in the subjects. However, they often overlooked the profound and successful roles the system had played for centuries in maintaining China's unique social, political, and cultural order. Its abolition in 1905, while necessary for modernization, also led to significant social upheaval, as it removed the long-standing path to social mobility and the unifying ideological glue of the empire.
