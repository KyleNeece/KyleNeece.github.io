**Main Point**: Helen Foster Snow was a journalist who took on an activist role in China during her time spent abroad there. She existed in a time period where American foreigners did not have to worry about the multidimensional conflict that occurred during the time of the missionary action in the time period we previously just looked at. Further, she also existed in a time period where women didn't necessarily have the most rights out of the bunch, which limited her contributions from her own perspective.

**Point 1**
- Her life story intersected with the previous readings we have had this week. While most missionaries wanted to help with different East Asian Countries problems, they ultimately couldn't because they were deep rooted in colonial violence. Helen, as a journalist, was not so much caught in this problem, as she wanted to go to China for the purposes of recording a story and a history in order to educate the world. Furthermore, Helen had a lot more mobility then the missionaries did, allowing for her to be more mobile and cover more ground regarding her recordings and objectives. 

**Point 2**
- Helen's relationship to the Chinese revolution stems from the fact that she made a difference in where she was able to document the lives and actions of the Chinese revolution, to show the rest of the world that China was evolving and would not longer be the "inferior" people that they were seen as during her time there. She also helped with the student movement which revolutionized how the people of China were educated  

**Point 3**
- The most significant part of Helen's career was not necessarily the impact she had on the people she interacted with in the Chinese world, not for her ability to write a famous manuscript. The relationships she built in the connections she honed allowed for her to be immortalized in Chinese society forever.
