**Introduction Section**
- Very few African Americans, for historical reasons, participated in the cultural and educational exchange between the two countries.
- The PRC earned admiration from almost all of the African-American visitors and spread the teachings of the “Little Red Book” of Chairman Mao Zedong to black com- munities and college campuses in the United States, but it was unable to turn the civil-rights movement into an armed revolution capable of destroying the stronghold of the capitalist world.
- The examination of the rise and dissipation of the black bridge helps reveal the breadth of the Sino-American Cold War confrontation and the drastic changes in the nature, pattern, and function of cultural exchange between the two countries.

**Creating New Patterns**
- As the black visitors became prominent figures in Sino-American cultural interactions, racial inequality in the United States became the center of attention, and China served as a source of inspiration and guidance.
	- Although white Americans who visited China during this period still outnumbered their black counterparts, the increase in the number of African Americans who came to China was unprecedented.
- “I never felt the touch or breath of insult or even dislike—I who for ninety years in America scarcely ever saw a day without some expression of hate for ‘niggers.’”
- Having been steeped in the civil-rights movement, the African-American visitors not only perceived China through the “colored lens” but also presented the United States to the Chinese people with a sharp focus on its notorious race relations.
- Seeing the United States in such a negative light, the African-American activists—unlike most of their white predecessors, who had worked hard to introduce American ideas, values, religion, science, technology, education, art, and movies to the Chinese people—did not believe their country had anything to offer China.
- Inspired by the Chinese Communist leaders’ writings and their revolutionary experiences, Williams intensified his criticism of the non-violent movement led by Martin Luther King and heightened his call for guerrilla warfare in the United States.
- All the African-American activists were carefully chosen by the Chinese authorities based mostly on three criteria: their favorable attitudes toward the PRC, their hostile relations with the U.S. government, and their level of influence in their communities and professions.
- Williams enjoyed the warmest welcome and stayed the longest in China partly because, as the leader of an armed defense movement, he was trying to elude arrest by the Federal Bureau of Investigation (FBI), and partly because he openly praised Mao as the leader of the world revolution and attacked Moscow’s opposition to armed resistance.
- Much of the PRC’s sway over the African-American activists was achieved through Chinese officials and interpreters assigned to host the visitors.
- The Chinese government covered the travel expenses of all the African-American activists except Worthy.
- During the Cultural Revolution, Beijing regarded jazz as bourgeois music and thus banned it everywhere, despite the fact that this music genre had originated in the black communities in New Orleans and had been widely enjoyed by African Americans.
	- Banning different American Culture 
- Mao emphasized the barring of black students from Central High School and the bloodbath the authorities caused in Mississippi, but he ignored the changes brought about by those confrontations: the eventual integration of public schools in Arkansas, the repeal of segregation laws in cities and states, and the opening of universities to black students.
- At the national meeting on foreign affairs held in April 1958, Premier Zhou Enlai had instructed attendees that the defeat of the capitalist camp would require appeals to the people in the imperialist countries, “inspiring them to move toward socialism and rise for the revolution in their own country.”

**Reaping Mixed Results**
- Beijing began the transnational interactions in the 1950s to break the isolation imposed by the U.S. government and challenge its authority at home as well as abroad.
	- Although many African-American activists drew inspiration from the Chinese revolution, challenged the U.S. government, intensified their fight against racial discrimination, and won major victories, the civil-rights movement maintained its tradition as a predominantly peaceful struggle, and the stronghold of the capitalist world remained strong.
- William Dwight, president of the American Newspaper Publishers Association, sent a telegram to President Dwight Eisenhower, Vice President Richard Nixon, and Speaker of the House of Representatives Sam Rayburn protesting the State Department’s restriction on travel to mainland China and demanding the government’s respect for the freedom of U.S. jour- nalists to “travel to any country in the world with which the United States is not at war.”
- The reason Chinese leaders refused to accept U.S. journalists is not that they were obtuse but that they were not really interested in reestablishing normal cultural ties with the United States at that time.
- President Johnson, like most of his colleagues in the State Department, believed that Beijing’s goal for cultural exchange was to win recognition from the United States and admission to the United Nations (UN).
- To Mao, the rapid development of the African-American fight against racial discrimination reflected the intensification of class struggle in the United States.
- Mao wanted African Americans to learn a lesson from the assassination of King, a nonviolent leader, and see the contradictions between them and the U.S. ruling class as those between two opposing classes. Therefore, Mao emphasized that the only way African Americans could “win their complete liberation was by overthrowing the reactionary rule of the American monopolistic capitalist class and destroying the colonial and imperialist systems.”
- Williams claimed that he saluted the glorious Chinese brothers and the mighty Chinese People’s Republic on behalf of “all revolutionary American people,” especially “the brutally oppressed and victimized Afro-American freedom fighters.”
- Not all African-American activists received Mao’s support and guidance without reservation or opposition.
- Mao’s effort to turn the mostly non-violent civil-rights movement into an armed revolution failed to win strong and lasting support from African- American activists mostly because of their increasing doubts about Chinese- style revolution and their strong belief in the U.S. Constitution.

**Letting it fade away section**
- Despite strenuous interference from the FBI, Williams returned to the United States in September 1969. His departure from China marked the beginning of the decline of the Cold War transnational interaction between the PRC and African-American activists.
- Although Beijing as well as Washington had mixed feelings about Williams’s return to the United States, both tried to take advantage of his decision.
- However, Williams had trouble providing accurate information and in- depth analysis before as well as after his return to the United States.
- The restoration of normal cultural relations between the PRC and the United States offered both opportunities and challenges for African-American activists.
- Mao believed that Williams was in a good position to know that Soviet revisionist military pressure on China “has been on the increase and their activities to cause internal disintegration targeted against us have come to a shameless degree.”
- Although Williams later claimed that Mao’s letter was forged by the FBI to turn him against the Chinese leader, the meeting with Chinese reporters never took place.
	- Williams’s success in the case proved again that African-Americans could defend their legal and civil rights within the existing legal system.
- The PRC’s close interaction with an unprecedentedly large number of African-American activists during the Cold War not only extended the con- frontation between the two countries to the cultural front but also drasti- cally changed the nature, pattern, and function of Sino-American cultural exchanges.
- The close relationship that developed between the PRC and the black activists clearly demonstrates that the cultural isolation imposed by Washington was ineffective in undermining an authoritarian regime and that the restrictive transnational interaction under Beijing’s tight control was even more counterproductive.
